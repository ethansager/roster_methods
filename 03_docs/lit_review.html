1 Introduction

This document summarizes key literature related to household roster design choices in surveys. The goal is to inform the design and implementation of household rosters in household surveys by reviewing existing evidence on how different design choices impact data quality, respondent burden, and survey costs. Furthermore, it aims to identify gaps in the literature where additional research is needed.

2 Proposed Structure for Literature Review

The literature review will be organized around the following thematic tables:

  • T1: Overview of module design choices and key questions/variables

  • T2: Descriptive statistics on differences in key variables between surveys that made different design choices

  • T3: Time-cost by survey design choices

  • T4: Review of survey experiment literature to inform bias-cost tradeoffs.

3 T1: Overview of Module Design Choices

Design Choice Purpose Typical response options / format Evidence
Household definition Define who counts as a household member Resident, non-resident, consumes meals, contributes income (Beaman and Dillon 2012)
Respondent Who provides roster information Household head, spouse, knowledgeable adult, proxy (Masselus and Fiala 2024; Bardasi et al. 2011; Sharma, Swaminathan, and Lahoti 2025)
Probes To ellict confirmation and mitigate coverage errors Probing on defintion, understanding of household realtionship (Tourangeau et al. 1997; Sweet 1994)

Three main design choices are highlighted household definition, respondent selection, and use of probes. Each choice has implications for data quality and comparability across surveys. First on the use of household definitions, Beaman and Dillon (2012) found that varying definitions can lead to significant differences in household size and composition 1. Second we highlight the choice of respondent in most household surveys this is a single respondent whom provides information on behalf of the entire household, Given that the respresntaion of a population at oindivual level is not always the goal of these surveys we can skip the debates around selection and representation. However we should keep in mind even when we are not worried about selection we should still consider the implications of respondent choice on data quality. Masselus and Fiala (2024) and Bardasi et al. (2011) show that the choice of respondent (spouse vs head and proxy vs self-report) affects the accuracy of reported household characteristics, however this is an area where cost tradeoffs and study purpose are paramount. Finally, probing has been studied mostly in the US context by Sweet (1994) and Tourangeau et al. (1997) which demonstrate that probing techniques can reduce underreporting of household members by clarifying definitions for respondents, however this is another burdensome activity that has not been fully tested in the developing context. Beaman and Dillon (2012) can be seen as a first step in this direction, but addtioanl probes beyond household definition were not tested.
